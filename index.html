THE

INCA EMPIRE

The Inca Empire, also called Tawantinsuyu (the Four Parts), was the "largest, most centralized polity of the pre-Columbian Americas" (Cuellar, 2022). The empire spanned a time period of roughly 1430 to 1532, ending when the empire was invaded by Spanish forces. The Inca Empire was located generally around the Andes mountains and the northwestern coasts of South America, in what now comprises regions of Ecuador, Peru, Chile, and Bolivia. The empire encompassed a multitude of ethnic groups, and was preceded politically and culturally by various smaller-scale nations.

The Inca Empire is variously notable for its sophisticated taxation system (despite a lack of a writing system), relatively unobtrusive imperial rule, wealth, mortuary practices, complex politics, and relationships with the natural world. Inca influence persists in modern-day Peru and surrounding states, from architecture to influences on indigenous identity.

GEOGRAPHY

The geographical context of the Andes region strongly influenced the culture, politics, and religion of the Inca Empire.

AGRICULTURE

The Incas utilized a unique agricultural system in the widely varying biomes found in the Andean region.

RELIGION

Mortuary practices, ancestor veneration, and worship of the sun and moon impacted the politics and social structure of the Inca Empire.
